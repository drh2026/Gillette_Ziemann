The White House Cook Book

A Comprehensive Cyclopedia of Information for the Home

Mrs. F.L. Gillette & Hugo Ziemann  ·  Chicago, 1887

The Work

The White House Cook Book was first published in 1887 by the Chicago firm of R.S. Peale & Co. and became one of the best-selling American cookbooks of the nineteenth century, running to dozens of editions over the following four decades. Co-authored by Fanny Lemira Gillette (1828–1926), a domestic economy writer, and Hugo Ziemann, steward of the White House under President Grover Cleveland, it combined Gillette's tested household recipes with Ziemann's professional kitchen authority.

It is dedicated to the wives of American presidents — "those noble women who have graced the White House" — and opens with portraits and menus from the presidential household, lending the title its authority and its remarkable commercial success.

Scope & Structure

The book is organised as a cyclopedia in thirty chapters, spanning the full range of the American household table in the 1880s: soups, fish, poultry and game, meats, vegetables, salads, bread, eggs, butter and cheese, macaroni, pastry and pies, cakes, custards, ice cream, puddings, pickles and catsups, confectionery, canned fruits, and beverages. The non-culinary sections — carving diagrams, toilet recipes, household management, health suggestions, and White House etiquette — are excluded from this portal.

This portal contains 1,131 recipes from the culinary sections, drawn from the Cornell University Library first edition (1887), available on Archive.org.

Mrs. F.L. Gillette

Fanny Lemira Gillette was one of the most commercially successful American food writers of the Gilded Age. She made a lifelong study of cookery and housekeeping, with particular attention to the practical wants of average American homes — a practical orientation that distinguishes the White House Cook Book from the more genteel domestic literature of the era.

Hugo Ziemann's professional credentials as White House steward gave the book its distinctive dual character: it speaks simultaneously to the domestic manager and the aspirational dinner-giver, its recipes equally at home on a farm table or at a state dinner.

Historical Context

The White House Cook Book appeared in 1887 — the same year the archive's La Cuisine Créole was published by Lafcadio Hearn in New Orleans, two years after Maria Parloa's New Cook Book (1882) and Marion Harland's Common Sense in the Household (1883) had established the market for practical American domestic cookery. Where Parloa and Harland addressed the New England and middle-Atlantic household, Gillette and Ziemann aimed at a national readership, and the White House credential gave them a reach no rival could match.

The book documents the American kitchen at a precise cultural moment: the end of the wood-burning stove era, the beginning of systematic canning, the incorporation of French professional technique into the domestic repertoire, and the codification of a distinctly American festive table — Thanksgiving, the presidential reception, the church supper. Its recipe categories — from beef soup to bourbon whiskey cake, from lobster salad to corn meal mush — constitute a nearly complete map of what Americans ate in the Gilded Age.

Within the Historical Culinary Archive, Gillette fills the 1885–1889 gap in the American Classics sequence, bridging Hearn's Creole survey (1885) and Thomas Filippini's Delmonico's record (The Table, 1889).

In the Wild — Gillette's Recipes on Historical Menus

The New York Public Library's What's on the Menu? project has digitised over 17,500 menus from American restaurants spanning 1843 to the present, with more than 1.3 million transcribed dish records from the period 1840–1930. These records document dishes that appeared in actual commercial dining — prescriptions made real.

Copyright Status

The White House Cook Book (1887) is in the universal public domain. Published over 135 years ago, copyright protection has long expired in all territories worldwide, including the United States (published before 1928).
